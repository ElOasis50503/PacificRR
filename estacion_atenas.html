Km 37.3 — a heritage station on the steepest, most engineering-intensive stretch of the Pacific line, photographed at its original wooden building a decade before fire destroyed it, alongside a quieter look at the town it served.

About this spoke: Station No. 19 on the Pacific line stood not in the town of Atenas itself but in the separate hamlet of Río Grande, in the Concepción district of Atenas canton — a road was specifically built in 1903 to connect the two. This spoke gathers two dated photographs (26 January 1923) of that original wooden station building and its adjoining water tower, alongside a separate, undated postcard view of a street in the town of Atenas proper. The two localities are kept honestly distinct, since the surviving captions support only that distinction, not a single combined scene.

The Station, 1923

ATE-001 26 January 1923

La Estación de Río Grande, Vista del Tanque

Station building and water tower, with workers gathered on the platform

A long, single-story wooden station building runs back from the camera under a corrugated-iron roof, its covered platform crowded with figures in broad-brimmed hats standing or walking near a string of boxcars on the siding. Beside the station stands a cylindrical water tower of riveted steel plate, raised on a rough fieldstone masonry base with a wooden door set into the stonework — the kind of trackside water stop a steam locomotive needed regularly on the climb between the coast and the Central Valley. The photograph is inscribed by hand at upper left, "Atenas, 1/26/23," giving this plate a firm date.

This building is not the structure that survives today as the Museo Ferroviario de Atenas. Period sources record that the original wooden station at Río Grande burned completely in 1933, and that the building currently standing — the one restored as a museum, opened to the public in 2001 — dates from the rebuilding that followed, in the mid-1930s. The two plates gathered on this spoke therefore document a structure that no longer exists, photographed roughly a decade before its loss.

ATE-002 26 January 1923

La Estación de Río Grande, Vista de los Edificios

Station outbuildings and platform, looking along the line

A companion view of the same date and locality, taken from further along the track: a row of wooden buildings on stilted foundations recedes into the distance along a covered platform with a decorative crisscross balustrade, the structures growing progressively smaller and less distinct toward a stand of bare-branched trees at the vanishing point. The inscription "Atenas" is legible at the lower right corner of the print, partially cropped, matching the same hand and the same 1/26/23 date as the companion plate above.

Together, the two photographs record both ends of the same small station complex on a single day in January 1923 — the water-tower end, where locomotives took on water for the climb, and this row of platform-side outbuildings further down the line. Both depict the pre-1933 wooden station lost to fire.

A Street in Atenas

ATE-003 Date unconfirmed

Una Calle, Atenas

Printed postcard, town street, undated

A commercial, bilingually captioned postcard — "Una calle / Atenas / A street," printed beneath the image in the trade convention common to the period — shows an unpaved street receding toward a vanishing point lined with low single-story buildings on the left and a taller, two-story wooden building with a balcony on the right, where two figures in pale clothing walk near the foreground. A solitary palm tree stands roughly at the street's midpoint, and a low fence or hedge is visible near the corner building's entrance.

This card is kept deliberately separate from the two Río Grande station plates above. Nothing in its caption ties it to the railway, the station hamlet, or a specific year, and the printed format and bilingual convention typify a tourist or souvenir postcard rather than a personal or institutional photograph. It documents the town of Atenas itself — the settlement the road of 1903 was built to connect to its own railway station — rather than the station complex.

Atenas and Río Grande: A Station Built for a Town That Wasn't There

Atenas presents an unusual case among the Pacific line's heritage stations, because the station and the town it served were never quite the same place. When planning reached this stretch of the route in the early 1900s, the railway's own siting requirements — a workable grade, water for the locomotives, room for a yard — put the stop at Río Grande, a separate hamlet some distance from the town of Atenas itself. A connecting road had to be built specifically in 1903 to link the two, a detail that survives in municipal records but rarely makes it into casual accounts of the line.

The wooden station built at Río Grande followed the standard period plan — platform, corridors, waiting room, office, warehouse — and went into informal use even before the full San José–Puntarenas line was complete, since this segment as far as Río Grande was already operating by the close of the Rafael Iglesias Castro administration around 1902. That first building did not survive the line's working life: a fire destroyed it completely in 1933, and the structure standing today — restored as the Museo Ferroviario de Atenas, officially opened in September 2001 and declared Patrimonio Histórico-Arquitectónico in January 2003 — dates only from the mid-1930s rebuilding. The two dated photographs gathered on this spoke, taken in January 1923, are therefore a record of a building that no longer exists, predating the fire by roughly a decade.

A few kilometers from the station, on the boundary between Turrúcares and Concepción de Atenas, the Puente Río Grande carries its own substantial engineering history, designed by the American civil engineer Theodore Cooper and fabricated by Milliken Bros. of New York, construction beginning in 1901 and the bridge entering service in March 1902 — among the most demanding crossings on the entire Pacific line's descent from the Central Valley. That crossing, and a companion construction-phase photograph of it, are catalogued separately under this hub's Río Grande bridge entry, cross-referenced to the Transport Costa Rica Histórica series.
