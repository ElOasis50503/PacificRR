Estanque de Pozón

Water reservoir · Station No. 32 · km 74.0 · Puntarenas Province

The caption at the bottom reads Estanque de Pozón. No date or plate number is visible on this image, though its presence in the same survey collection as the January–May 1923 plates places it within that documented programme. An estanque is a holding tank or reservoir — in railway terms, the water supply for locomotive replenishment. This image documents not the station building but the hydraulic infrastructure that made Pozón a functioning stop on the Pacific lowland run.

The photograph shows a long, low concrete or masonry cistern occupying the middle ground, its flat coping clearly defined against the surface of the water it holds. The tank appears to be set slightly below grade on the far side, with the near wall rising perhaps 60–80 cm above the surrounding ground level — a gravity-fed design that would allow water to be drawn from a tap or pipe at the base by locomotive tenders standing alongside. The water surface is still and mirror-like, reflecting the tree canopy above; reeds and aquatic marginals grow along the near edge where the tank has apparently been allowed to overflow or where groundwater seeps in.

Behind the cistern, in the middle distance, a tall cylindrical masonry structure rises above the vegetation — this is almost certainly a water tower or standpipe, likely of brick or dressed stone, used to elevate the reservoir above track level to provide pressure by gravity head alone. The trees surrounding it are largely leafless, consistent with the dry season. The overall scene has a slightly overgrown quality: the tank is functional but not meticulously maintained, with vegetation encroaching on its margins. At right, a large boulder or stone outcrop is visible, suggesting the cistern was sited where natural rock provided a stable foundation or where a spring emerged.

Pozón (station No. 32, km 74.0) sits in the lowland zone of Puntarenas Province, where the Pacific Railway had descended fully from the Central Valley escarpment and was running through the coastal plain. At this point on the line — between Mastate (No. 30, km 69.3) and Coyolar (No. 31, km 71.0) to the north, and Muñoz (No. 33, km 76.0) to the south — reliable water supply would have been essential for steam locomotives completing the long run toward Puntarenas or beginning the climb back toward San José. The estanque here served that need.
