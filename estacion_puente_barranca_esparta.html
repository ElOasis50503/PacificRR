The Bridge, 1923

PBARRANCA-001 17 January 1923

Puente Barranca, Ramal Esparta

Broadside view from the dry riverbed

A long steel through-truss bridge spans the Río Barranca on a shallow arched profile, carried at the near end on a coursed-masonry pier rising from the riverbank and continuing on a matching pier at the far end, just visible among the trees. The riverbed itself is almost entirely dry, filled with rounded stones and gravel from bank to bank — a dry-season view that exposes the full masonry foundation of the crossing down to the waterline. Dense riverine forest crowds both banks and rises behind the bridge on the far side, framing the structure against an overcast sky. The print is captioned by hand, "Puente Barranca, Ramal Esparta," dated 17 January 1923, and numbered 106 in the corner.

PBARRANCA-002 17 January 1923

Puente Barranca, Ramal Esparta

Deck-level view looking down the truss

The companion plate to the broadside view, taken from the bridge's own deck looking down its length: weathered wooden planking and rail ties run between riveted steel verticals and diagonal lattice bracing, with a portal frame in the foreground and a second, fainter portal visible deep in the span beyond. A single figure walks the track partway across, giving scale to the structure's considerable length. Wooden handrails line both sides of the walkway, and the surrounding hillside, cut back in steep earthen banks at the bridge's near end, frames the view on both flanks. The print carries the same caption and date as its companion — "Puente Barranca, Ramal Esparta," 17 January 1923 — and is numbered 105.

Together, the two plates document the crossing from outside and from within: the broadside view establishing the bridge's setting in the dry Barranca riverbed, and this deck-level view recording the riveted ironwork and timber decking of the structure itself, on the branch line that long predates the numbered Pacific trunk sequence.

The Ramal Esparta: Costa Rica's Second Railway

Long before William Lynn signed his 1895 contract to build the numbered Pacific trunk line, Costa Rica had already tried, twice, to lay rails toward Puntarenas. The first attempt, contracted to the English entrepreneur Richard Farrer in 1854 and opened on 12 November 1857, covered just 15 km between Puntarenas and Barranca — the original "burrocarril," its cars hauled by mules at roughly three kilometers per hour, humorously named for its motive power. Underfinanced and unable to extend further inland, the line lasted barely a year before collapsing, though it had already proven the basic case for a railway linking the port to the interior.

A second attempt fared better. On 20 April 1882, the government signed a contract with Juan Myer(s) to complete the stretch from Barranca to Esparza within a year, extending the old Farrer alignment using equipment supplied by Minor Keith's Northern Railway Company — the same firm then building the Atlantic line out of Limón. This Ramal Esparta (also called Línea Vieja in some later sources) thus predates the modern numbered Pacific sequence by well over a decade, and for a time it operated as a self-financing branch in its own right: a later, ultimately unsuccessful scheme under Minister of Fomento Alejandro Alvarado attempted to extend it inland via San Ramón, Naranjo, and San Antonio de Belén as an alternative Pacific route, but the contractors Cyril Smith and Cooper failed to deliver, leaving the Esparta branch to fund its own operations while the government's attention turned to Lynn's trunk-line contract.

When the modern numbered sequence was eventually built and electrified, it bypassed the old branch's two intermediate stations — No. 48 (La Ligia) and No. 49 (Esparta) — which this catalogue's main station table omits for that reason. The bridge in these two plates, photographed on 17 January 1923, is one of the surviving structures from that earlier era: a steel through-truss crossing of the Río Barranca, built to a design and scale matching the riveted ironwork seen elsewhere on the line's bridges, but standing on a route that the rest of the system had already passed over in favor of newer engineering further inland. The two companion photographs — one from the dry riverbed below, one from the timber decking above — together preserve both the setting and the structure of a crossing that connects directly back to Costa Rica's first attempts at a Pacific railway in the 1850s and 1880s.
