La Angostura — Locomotora No. 10

Locomotive No. 10 at the coastal narrows · between Chacarita and Puntarenas · c. 1900–1910

The filename Near_Puntarenas is the only inscription associated with this image. The location is clearly La Angostura — the “narrows” — the celebrated section of the Pacific Railway where the line squeezed between the cliff face of the Puntarenas peninsula and the waters of the Gulf of Nicoya in the final approach to the port. The hub table for this catalogue notes La Angostura in relation to Chacarita (No. 56, km 110.0), after which the narrows section precedes the terminus at Puntarenas (No. 58, km 116.0).

Locomotive No. 10 rounds the cliff face from the right, its headlamp, boiler, and smoke stack in silhouette against the light sky. Steam rises from the stack. Two figures are present: one man standing on the running board or footplate at the front of the locomotive, braced with one hand on the boiler, facing the camera; a second man in a formal dark suit and hat stands on the ground to the left of the locomotive, posing in a supervisory or official stance. The ground between the locomotive and the cliff base is strewn with broken rock — construction or maintenance debris, with freshly spalled material from the cliff visible at the base.

The cliff itself is the dominant compositional element: a massive vertical face of weathered volcanic or sedimentary rock, deeply fissured and partially undercut by wave action or weathering, rising well above the locomotive’s height. The track runs on a narrow ledge cut into or along the base of this cliff, with no margin visible between the track centreline and the cliff face to the left. To the right of the track, beyond the righthand rail, the land drops away immediately to the Gulf of Nicoya — its flat grey surface visible in the background extending to a low coastline horizon. This is one of the most spatially dramatic sections of any Central American railway: the line literally clung to a cliff above the sea.

The locomotive appears to be a small early American-type steam engine, consistent with the Pacific Railway’s early operational fleet acquired before the government-ownership period. The number 10 is legible on the smokebox door. The formal attire of the ground-level figure and the deliberate posing suggest this may be a documentation or promotional photograph made during an early inspection or inaugural run rather than a casual operational image.
