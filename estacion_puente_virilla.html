Near Pavas, station No. 3 on the Pacific line — a different river and a different publisher from the line's more famous Río Grande crossing, but carrying something the Paynter Bros. postcards do not: a named individual's own account of having built it.

About these plates: This page sits outside the Pacific line's numbered station catalogue — kept separate, standalone, because of what makes its first plate distinctive: a private, handwritten inscription naming the engineer who oversaw its masonry construction. Published by Anto Lehmann's bookshop in San José under the "Memorias de Costa Rica" series, that card carries three printed views of the Río Virilla crossing near Pavas (station No. 3), and a personal note from a writer identifying himself only as "Guillermo." A second plate, a separately sourced and precisely dated working photograph of the same bridge from 12 January 1923, is gathered alongside it.

The Card

VIRILLA-001 c. 1900–1906

Puente sobre el Río Virilla

Memorias de Costa Rica · Ferrocarril al Pacífico, with a personal inscription

A three-view souvenir card under the heading "Ferrocarril al Pacífico — Memorias de Costa Rica," published by Anto Lehmann's bookshop in San José. The upper left panel, captioned by hand "Puente sobre el río 'Vinilla,'" shows the steel trestle bridge crossing the Virilla gorge, photographed from trackside looking down the line; the upper right oval vignette shows a wider view of a deep rail cutting through hillside, with workers and a small rail car visible on the grade below; the lower panel shows a steam locomotive and a single passenger/baggage car standing on the line, with a crew of roughly fifteen to twenty men posed in front for the camera, top-hatted supervisors at the engine alongside laborers in shirtsleeves.

Beneath the printed title "Ferrocarril al Pacífico / Memorias de Costa Rica," the card carries a handwritten inscription in period Spanish script: "La obra de mampostería del puente sobre el Vinilla la dirigió tu afmo. hno. Guillermo" — "The masonry work on the bridge over the Virilla was directed by your affectionate brother, Guillermo." The writer, Guillermo, was almost certainly among the men posed in the lower photograph, and the card itself was very likely sent home as a personal record of his own work on the crossing — a private keepsake riding on a commercially printed souvenir.

VIRILLA-002 12 January 1923

Puente Virilla

A separate photographic record, distinct from the Anto Lehmann souvenir card

An oblique view of the same Río Virilla crossing, taken from below and to one side of the structure: a riveted steel through-truss, its diagonal lattice bracing rising toward a portal frame at the far end, carried on a deck whose underside and supporting piers are visible in the lower portion of the frame. A wooden tripod-like structure, possibly survey equipment or a work scaffold, stands at the base of the nearer pier, and a single figure is visible standing on the high embankment at the far right. Dense scrub covers the slopes below the bridge on both sides. The print is captioned by hand, "Puente Virilla," dated 12 January 1923, and numbered 13.

This plate is a separate documentary record from the Anto Lehmann souvenir card above — a different angle, a different photographer's hand, and a precise date rather than the souvenir card's undated commercial printing. Where the Lehmann card frames the bridge as a finished landmark worth commemorating in a printed series, this plate reads as working documentation: a close, structural view of the kind a railway survey or maintenance record might have produced, predating the souvenir card by some years if the broader 1900–1906 estimate for that card's printing is correct.

Guillermo's Bridge

Most of the railway postcards in this collection are commercial products pure and simple: a photographer's studio printed a striking view, a publisher distributed it, and a buyer mailed it on or kept it as a souvenir, with no personal connection to the infrastructure pictured. This card from Anto Lehmann's "Memorias de Costa Rica" series breaks that pattern. Somewhere beneath the printed title, a man named Guillermo took up a pen and wrote to a sibling that he had personally directed the masonry work — la obra de mampostería — on the bridge shown in the card's own photographs, very likely placing himself among the men posed in front of the locomotive in the lower panel.

That single sentence turns a piece of railway-company marketing into a primary document of personal engineering biography. Masonry work on a structure like this — the abutments, piers, and any retaining work supporting the steel trestle visible in the card's upper-left view — required skilled supervision distinct from the steel erection itself, the kind of work an engineer or foreman might reasonably claim credit for in a letter home. Unlike the Río Grande bridge elsewhere in this catalogue, whose construction and completed form are documented only by anonymous commercial photography, the Virilla crossing comes with a name attached to at least one part of its construction — a small but genuinely rare thing among railway ephemera of this period, where the men who built these structures are almost never identified by the postcards that made their work look picturesque.

The crossing itself sits near Pavas, station No. 3 on the Pacific line's numbered sequence, not far past the San José terminus and on a gentler stretch of grade than the dramatic gorge crossings further along the route toward Atenas. Its modest scale relative to Río Grande may be exactly why this card survives with its inscription intact: a structure important enough to photograph and sell as a souvenir, but personal enough still that the man who helped build it felt it was his own to write home about.
