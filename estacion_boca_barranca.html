Km 97 — the Boca del Río Barranca crossing in María Baja, where the Pacific line's track ran along the exposed shoreline before crossing the river mouth on a low timber pile trestle, repeatedly damaged by the seasonal floods and high tides that plagued this final stretch into Puntarenas.

About this spoke: Boca Barranca — the mouth of the Río Barranca, in the locality of María Baja near Puntarenas — was one of the most flood-exposed points on the entire Pacific line. The Archivo Nacional de Costa Rica catalogues a related photograph of "puente ferroviario de la Boca del río La Barranca en María baja, construído sobre pilotes de madera" — a wooden pile-trestle bridge built after the 1916 floods destroyed an earlier crossing — and this catalogue's print 5985-2 is itself cited by accession number in published scholarship on flood risk along the Caldera–Puntarenas stretch of the line. This spoke gathers all four plates from a single January 1923 documentary excursion: bare trackwork laid directly along the water's edge, the pile-trestle bridge at low tide ("Boca, 1/27/1923") and at high tide eleven days earlier ("Puente Boca en Pleamar, 1/16/23"), and a wider view of the small settlement that grew up beside the crossing.

The Crossing, 1923

BOCA-001 January 1923

Trackwork at the Water's Edge

Print no. 95 — the exposed approach to Boca Barranca

A single rail line runs along a narrow ballast bed laid directly at the waterline, the rail joints and tie plates exposed in the foreground while a worker crouches over the track in the middle distance, attending to the line. Open water fills most of the frame to the right, with a low wooded shore closing the view beyond. The exposed, low-lying roadbed visible here illustrates exactly why this stretch of the approach to Puntarenas was so vulnerable to tidal flooding and storm damage — the same vulnerability that the pile-trestle bridge in the next plate was built to answer. The print is numbered 95 in the corner.

BOCA-002 27 January 1923

Boca, the Pile Trestle

Print no. 96 — the rebuilt crossing at the river mouth

A long, low timber pile-and-stringer trestle stretches across calm tidal water, photographed from a low vantage point through dense mangrove scrub in the foreground. Repeated rows of paired wooden piles, driven directly into the riverbed, carry a simple plank deck and rail across the wide, shallow mouth of the river — a deliberately repetitive, low-cost construction suited to a tidal inlet rather than the single-span steel trusses seen at Río Grande and Río Barranca on the Ramal Esparta. A sandbar and the gulf's open water are visible beyond the far end of the bridge. The print is captioned by hand, "Boca, 1/27/1923," and numbered 96.

This plate is independently cited by its archival accession number (5985-2) in published Costa Rican scholarship on flood risk along the Caldera–Puntarenas stretch of the Pacific line, which identifies the structure as the railway bridge over the Boca del Río Barranca in María Baja, rebuilt on wooden piles after an earlier crossing was destroyed by flooding in 1916.

BOCA-003 16 January 1923

Puente Boca en Pleamar

The same crossing at high tide, eleven days before BOCA-002

The same low timber trestle as the preceding plate, photographed eleven days earlier at high tide: the water has risen well up the piles, leaving only the upper third of the trestle's substructure clear of the surface, where the low-tide view shows the full run of piles down to the waterline. A patch of mangrove scrub breaks the surface at left, and the same wooded rise closes the far bank. The print is captioned by hand, "Puente Boca en Pleamar" (Boca Bridge at high tide), dated 16 January 1923.

Paired with the 27 January low-tide plate, this photograph reads as a deliberate before-and-after pairing rather than two unrelated shots: the same structure, the same vantage point, captured at opposite points in the tidal cycle to document exactly how much of the crossing's substructure the Gulf of Nicoya's tides could submerge — useful evidence, in a documentary sense, for understanding why this stretch of line needed a rebuilt bridge in the first place.

Boca, the Settlement at the Crossing

Print no. 97 — companion wide view

A wider view across the same inlet, the trestle bridge entering from the left and several small wooden buildings standing on the sandy spit beyond it, set among scattered trees; a handful of figures are visible walking the track near the buildings. A further structure stands at the right edge of the frame, photographed at a steep oblique angle that foreshortens it against the dark tree line — on close inspection, a building similar to the others on the spit rather than storm wreckage, though the angle and the print's condition make it easy to mistake for damaged or collapsed structure at a glance. Boca Barranca: A Crossing Rebuilt by Necessity

Few stretches of the Pacific line suffered as much repeated damage as the low ground around the mouth of the Río Barranca, in the locality known as María Baja on the final approach to Puntarenas. The line here ran close to sea level, parallel to the coast, exposed at once to the river's seasonal floods and to the rising tides of the Gulf of Nicoya — a double vulnerability that period press coverage documented in vivid terms. On 10 October 1906, the newspaper La Prensa Libre reported a cyclone-driven disaster at the port, noting that "the fall of the Barranca bridge and the collapse of one of the abutments that supported it" was among the damages, alongside washed-out bridges elsewhere on the line and landslides that had already been a constant problem during construction.

That 1906 washout was not the last word. The Archivo Nacional de Costa Rica's own catalogue records that the railway bridge at the Boca del Río Barranca was destroyed again by flooding in 1916, after which it was rebuilt on wooden piles — the simple, low-cost pile-and-stringer construction visible in this spoke's second plate, photographed on 27 January 1923. Unlike the steel through-trusses at Río Grande and at Puente Barranca on the Ramal Esparta, a pile trestle of this kind could be rebuilt relatively quickly and cheaply after storm damage, at the cost of being less durable against the next flood — a pattern repeated often enough along this stretch that flood and tidal damage to the Caldera–Puntarenas line became a recurring subject of national reporting and, eventually, of academic study. The first plate in this spoke, showing bare track laid directly at the waterline with a worker crouched over a rail joint, captures the same vulnerability from a different angle: track with essentially no elevation above the tideline, the kind of alignment that all but guaranteed periodic washouts.

This spoke's full set of four plates, photographed across two visits eleven days apart, amounts to a small but careful documentary survey of a single crossing: bare track exposed at the waterline, the trestle itself at low tide on 27 January and at high tide eleven days earlier on 16 January, and a wider view taking in the small settlement of buildings that had grown up on the spit beside the bridge. Read together, the low-tide and high-tide pair makes a deliberate point about how much of the structure's substructure the Gulf of Nicoya's tides could submerge — exactly the kind of exposure that had already destroyed one bridge here in 1916 and would continue to test the railway's engineers for as long as trains ran this final stretch into Puntarenas.
