A street-level crossing where the rails of two different companies met — the Ferrocarril al Pacífico (F.C.P.) clearly labeled on one track, and a second line marked "F.C.C.R" on the other, photographed on 18 January 1923 with townspeople and buildings crowding both sides of the right-of-way.

About this spoke: This plate documents a genuinely uncommon subject within this catalogue: a junction between the Ferrocarril al Pacífico and a second line, captioned on the print itself with the abbreviations "F.C.P." and "F.C.C.R." The Pacific identification is certain — the line this entire supplement documents. The second abbreviation most plausibly refers to the Costa Rica Railway Company, the English firm that built and for decades operated the Atlantic line to Limón (commonly abbreviated C.R.R.C. in period company reports), though this catalogue has not been able to confirm the specific named locality of this junction or pin the abbreviation down with full certainty, and presents it honestly as an informed reading rather than settled fact.

The Junction, 1923

EMPALME-001 18 January 1923

Empalme F.C.C.R y F.C.P.

Where two railway companies' tracks crossed

A track-level view straight down a street, modest single-story houses lining both sides, with several figures standing or walking on and beside the rails in the middle distance. Two sets of track converge and cross near the foreground: the right-hand rail is hand-labeled "F.C.P.," curving off to the right, while the straight track running down the center of the frame is labeled "F.C.C.R." The print is captioned by hand, "Empalme F.C.C.R y F.C.P.," dated 18 January 1923, with an embossed seal at upper right and the number "1" written in the lower corner.

F.C.P. unambiguously stands for Ferrocarril al Pacífico, the line this entire documentary supplement covers. F.C.C.R most plausibly abbreviates the Costa Rica Railway Company — the English firm chartered in 1886 that built and for decades operated the Atlantic line to Limón, commonly rendered "C.R.R.C." in the company's own period reports — though this catalogue has not confirmed that reading with full certainty, nor identified the specific named locality where this junction stood. It is presented here as a genuinely uncommon subject: a photograph of the literal meeting point between Costa Rica's two national trunk lines, rather than a view of either line in isolation.

Where Two Railways Met

For most of the twentieth century, Costa Rica was served by two entirely separate national railways, built decades apart by different concessionaires for different purposes: the Ferrocarril al Atlántico, begun in 1871 and finally completed to Limón in 1890 under the Costa Rica Railway Company and later the Northern Railway Company, and the Ferrocarril al Pacífico, contracted to William Lynn in 1895 and completed to Puntarenas only in 1910. Both lines converged on San José, each with its own terminus building — the Atlantic station, built in 1908 to a design by architect Jaime Carranza Aguilar, and the Pacific station, rebuilt in 1941 by architect José Francisco Salazar Quesada — but for most of their working lives the two systems remained administratively and operationally distinct, run by different companies under different concession terms.

This photograph documents an unusually direct intersection of that divided system: a point where the rails of the two lines crossed or converged closely enough to be captured labeled side by side in a single frame, on 18 January 1923. The location itself is not confirmed by any source consulted for this catalogue — it is somewhere the two companies' rights-of-way met, photographed with the same documentary care given to the bridges and stations elsewhere in this 1923 survey, but without the kind of unambiguous geographic caption that anchors most of this collection's other plates. What the image makes visible, regardless of its exact location, is something easy to forget once both lines were folded into a single state railway: that for decades, riding from the Pacific coast to the Atlantic meant changing, in some practical sense, from one company's railway onto another's.
