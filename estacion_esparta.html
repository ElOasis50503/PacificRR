Esparta — Patio de la Estación

Station No. 49 (Línea Vieja) · Ramal Esparta · 27 January 1923

The inscription at the lower right reads Esparta 1/27/23. No plate number is visible in this image, but the date — eight days after Cascajal (plate 67, 19 January) and eleven days after the Corte Matamoros / Puente Jesús María pair (plates 69 and 71, 16 January) — places this squarely within the January 1923 survey sequence. The survey had evidently completed its southward run along the main line toward Puntarenas by mid-January and turned to document the Ramal Esparta branch by the 27th.

The photograph shows the station compound at Esparta from the trackside, looking northwest into the town. In the left foreground, the track bed is visible at the bottom of the frame, and a large pile of cut timber — cordwood staged for locomotive fuel — occupies the near ground against a stone retaining wall. The wall itself is substantial: rough-coursed volcanic stone, perhaps 1.5–2 metres high, running the width of the image and separating the railway yard level from the town above. This kind of terraced arrangement is characteristic of the Esparza district, where the town occupies a raised bench above the lower floodplain through which the railway ran.

Above the wall, the town of Esparta spreads across the middle ground. Two principal buildings are clearly legible: at left, a long single-storey structure with a low-pitched roof and what appears to be a continuous row of windows — possibly a school, municipal building, or substantial commercial warehouse — and at centre-right, a smaller building with a hipped roof and a broad-eaved veranda that may be the station building proper or a connected bodega. Additional structures are visible to the right, becoming less distinct in the dry-season haze. Mature trees — some of the largest visible in this collection’s January photographs — shade the compound and the street beyond.

Esparta was the commercial and administrative centre of the canton of Esparza, and the Ramal Esparta served it from El Roble on the main Pacific trunk line. The branch was already the older of the two routes into the Esparza district — it predated the main-line completion to El Roble in 1910 — and by 1923 was operating as a branch rather than a trunk, with the heavier traffic running through on the main line. The cordwood stockpile visible here is consistent with a fuelling stop on a branch where steam locomotives turned around for the return run to El Roble.
