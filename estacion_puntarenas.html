Km 116.0 — station No. 58, the final stop on the Pacific line and its original reason for being: a line built, above all else, to move coffee from the Central Valley down to deep water on the Gulf of Nicoya.

About this spoke: Puntarenas was the Pacific line's entire reason for existing: a route built, across nearly four decades of false starts and renewed contracts, to carry Central Valley coffee down to deep water on the Gulf of Nicoya. The terminus opened with the line's full 1910 inauguration and outlived it by decades, with the old customs house surviving today as the Universidad Técnica Nacional's Pacific campus. This spoke gathers two undated 1923 plates from the immediate terminus area: a rail-yard view with passenger coaches in the foreground, and a large two-story veranda house standing trackside near the port, whose specific identity is not confirmed by any source consulted for this catalogue.

PUNT-001 1923

Patio de Puntarenas

Rail yard at the terminus, passenger coaches in the foreground

Three passenger coaches stand coupled in the foreground, their curved roofs catching the light, while beyond them the yard opens onto a freight shed with a long covered platform and stacks of materials piled along the siding. Several rail spurs fan out across the yard toward the shed, and trees and low buildings close the view in the distance. The faint inscription "Puntarenas, Patio" is legible at upper left, with the print numbered 113 in the lower corner and an embossed seal visible at upper right — consistent with the same 1923 documentary excursion that produced this catalogue's other Puntarenas-approach plates.

PUNT-002 1923

Veranda House Near the Terminus

Two-story wood-frame building, print no. 112

An elaborate two-story wood-frame building stands close to the rail line, its wraparound verandas on both levels carried on turned columns and arched fretwork, the upper gallery enclosed by an ornate latticed balustrade. A whitewashed masonry ground-floor wall and a tall picket fence front the property, with several young royal palms planted along the fence line bending in a stiff onshore wind that has also caught the large palm at left. A smaller gabled structure, possibly a kiosk or gatehouse, stands just visible at the right edge of the frame, and the rail line itself runs along the foreground. The print is numbered 112 and carries the same embossed seal seen on this catalogue's other 1923 Puntarenas plates.

No signage or caption identifies the building, and this catalogue does not have a confirmed identification for it. Its scale and ornamental finish — far beyond a simple residence, with the kind of wide, decoratively balustraded corredores that period sources describe as characteristic of the substantial cement, wood, and tile houses Puntarenas families built once the railway arrived between 1870 and 1890 — are consistent with a prosperous private residence, club, or company building of that era, but no source consulted for this catalogue confirms a specific identity. It is presented here honestly as an unidentified structure photographed in the immediate vicinity of the terminus.

Puntarenas: The Line's Reason for Being

Everything about the Pacific line, from Richard Farrer's failed 1857 burrocarril to William Lynn's 1895 trunk-line contract, was ultimately aimed at one destination: deep water on the Gulf of Nicoya, where Central Valley coffee could be loaded for the European market without the costly three-day mule trek over the Cerro del Aguacate that the railway was built to replace. The terminus reached its modern form only in 1910, when the last numbered station was finally connected to San José, and the line's 1930 electrification gave the port a faster, higher-capacity connection for the three remaining decades of regular service before the system-wide 1995 closure.

Puntarenas itself had been transforming for decades before the rails arrived in their final form. Built on a narrow sand spit projecting into the gulf, the city moved beyond its early nineteenth-century cane-and-thatch housing as the railway's construction — proceeding in stages through the 1870s and 1880s — brought new wealth and new building materials: substantial houses of cement, wood, and tile with high ceilings to temper the heat, and wide corredores often finished with decoratively turned balustrades, of the kind visible on the veranda building in this spoke's second plate. The terminal itself, fronting the beach, was built along similar lines: airy structures with rectangular windows and ornamented corridors suited to a tropical port, serving as the gateway for the foreign tourists and Central Valley families alike who made the route between the station and Los Baños the city's most heavily trafficked stretch.

Much of that built fabric has since been repurposed rather than lost. The old customs house at the port, where the Pacific Railway's cargo was loaded and unloaded, now houses the Universidad Técnica Nacional's Pacific campus, while the 1938 Capitanía — a separate, later Art Deco building by architect José María Barrantes Monge, distinguished by its own small lighthouse — was restored in 2008 as a tourism center under the "Puntarenas por siempre" program. The veranda house photographed in this spoke's second plate has not been identified with the same confidence: its ornamental scale points to a private residence, club, or company building from the railway-boom decades, but no source consulted here confirms which. What both plates agree on is the working character of the terminus they document — a yard full of rolling stock and freight sheds on one hand, and the substantial private architecture the railway's arrival helped finance on the other, photographed within the same 1923 excursion that produced this catalogue's wider record of the line's final approach to port.
