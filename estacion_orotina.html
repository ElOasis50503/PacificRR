Km 66.3 — one of the line's five Patrimonio-protected stations, today an agricultural-supply warehouse and home to a restored Siemens locomotive, photographed here as a working freight yard a year before the 1924 earthquake reshaped the region.

About this spoke: Orotina is one of five stations on the Pacific line carrying formal Patrimonio Histórico-Arquitectónico protection. Unlike Atenas, where the original wooden station is documented as having burned in 1933, no equivalent loss is recorded for Orotina's heritage complex — the ticket office, passenger hall, warehouse, and machine house described in period architectural surveys, now serving as an agricultural-supply warehouse. This spoke gathers a single dated plate of the station yard, inscribed "Patio de Orotina, Set. 2, 1923" (2 September 1923), showing boxcars on the siding and the warehouse buildings a year before the 1924 earthquake that devastated the region.

The Yard, 1923

OROT-001 2 September 1923

Patio de Orotina

Station yard, boxcars, and warehouse buildings

Several converging tracks fan out across the yard in the foreground, with a covered boxcar mounted on a flatcar standing on the nearest siding and a second string of boxcars further along the line. Beyond the rolling stock, two gabled warehouse buildings with corrugated-iron roofs face the yard, framed by tall trees and a utility pole; a figure stands near the leftmost building, and two more walk along the platform toward the right edge of the frame. In the immediate foreground, the sloped roof edge of a covered platform shelters part of the frame, with a fragment of a station-name board, legible only as the letter "H," cropped at the picture's lower right. The photograph is inscribed by hand, "Patio de Orotina, Set. 2, 1923" (2 September 1923), with an archival number partially visible at the upper right.

Unlike the Río Grande station documented elsewhere on this hub, where fire destroyed the original wooden building in 1933, no comparable loss is recorded for Orotina's heritage complex. Period architectural surveys describe four linked structures — a reinforced-concrete ticket office in a rationalist idiom, a passenger hall, a warehouse, and a machine house — all built with the broad galvanized-iron eaves typical of the line's tropical stations. Whether the buildings visible in this 1923 plate are the same fabric the surveys describe isn't confirmed by any source consulted for this catalogue; what can be said is that the yard's general arrangement here — siding, warehouse, covered platform — matches the kind of complex that has anchored Orotina's station grounds for most of a century, photographed about a year before the 1924 Orotina earthquake (magnitude 7.0) devastated the surrounding region.

Orotina: A Station That Survived

Orotina's heritage station presents a different documentary problem than the Río Grande station elsewhere on this hub, where a 1933 fire and rebuild are explicitly recorded. No comparable event is documented for Orotina in the sources consulted here, but that absence of a record is not itself proof that the buildings standing today are the same fabric photographed in 1923 — a distinction this catalogue tries to hold onto rather than collapse into a tidier story. What is documented is the complex's general form: four linked structures — a reinforced-concrete ticket office and administrator's residence in a rationalist idiom, a passenger hall, a warehouse, and a machine house — sheltered under the broad galvanized-iron eaves the line's builders favored for the hot, humid lowland climate. The railway's place in local identity is commemorated today on the canton's own coat of arms.

The town's connection to the line owes much to one local figure: Primo Vargas Valverde, a transport operator turned landowner, donated the land for the station, the central plaza, the church of Santo Domingo, and the municipal market, and is remembered locally for having lobbied to bring the railway to Orotina after a visit from José Martí in 1894. The school built on land Vargas donated was later renamed in his honor, and Orotina's central park carries his name today.

This plate, inscribed "Patio de Orotina, Set. 2, 1923," captures the yard a little over a year before the magnitude-7.0 earthquake of 1924 that devastated the surrounding region and forced the rebuilding of much of the local economy, including the sawmill at the nearby Hacienda El Coyolar. The station itself weathered the line's eventual 1995 closure better than most: in 2017 the Asociación de Desarrollo para la Preservación del Patrimonio Cultural de Orotina arranged for one of the line's four Henschel Siemens electric locomotives — No. 130, part of a set of four delivered to Puntarenas in 1957 during the Figueres Ferrer administration's modernization of the Ferrocarril Eléctrico al Pacífico — to be relocated to the old warehouse for cosmetic restoration and display, giving the yard photographed here a working locomotive once again.
