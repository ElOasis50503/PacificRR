Estación Pavas, Today

A present-day photograph, not a period plate

A two-story timber building with a hipped roof and weathered teal-painted trim stands beside the old right-of-way, its ground floor lined with shuttered windows and doors along a covered walkway. A modern sign reading "PAVAS" is mounted on the side wall above a smaller sign that appears to read "P.B.A.S." or similar — likely a faded or partial rendering of the same name. Unlike every other photograph in this catalogue, which documents the Pacific line in the early-to-mid twentieth century, this image was taken in the present day and shows the building as it stands now, whatever its current use.

This catalogue cannot confirm whether the structure shown here is the original early-1900s station building or a later reconstruction on the same site; no source consulted gives a construction or rebuilding date for this specific structure. It is included as documentary evidence of what survives at Pavas today, set deliberately apart from this collection's period material rather than blended into it.

Pavas: One of the Line's First Stations

Pavas belongs to the earliest generation of stations built on the Pacific line, constructed during the first decade of the twentieth century alongside San José, Ojo de Agua, Nuestro Amo, Turrucares, and Hacienda Vieja, as William Lynn's original 1897 contract — and later Warren H. Knowlton's completion of the line in 1910 — pushed construction outward from the capital toward the coast. At km 6.3, it was one of the first stops a traveler leaving San José would have reached, not far from where the line crossed the Río Virilla on the steel trestle bridge documented separately in this catalogue's standalone Virilla page.

What makes this spoke different from the rest of the catalogue is its source material. Every other station and bridge gathered here is documented through period photographs, postcards, or souvenir cards from the railway's working decades. The single plate in this spoke is, by contrast, a present-day photograph — evidence not of how Pavas looked in 1905 or 1923, but of what stands on or near the site today: a weathered two-story timber building, clearly old, clearly disused as an active station, but still standing and still marked with the town's name. Whether this building dates to the line's original construction or was rebuilt at some later point is not something this catalogue can answer with the sources at hand; it is offered here honestly as an unresolved question, alongside the photograph itself as the best available record of the site's current state.
