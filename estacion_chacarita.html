The Angostura, 1923

CHACARITA-001 1923

Angostura, vista desde Chacarita

Calm-water view across the estuary narrows

A broad, still channel of water fills most of the frame, bounded on both sides by dense, low mangrove and scrub vegetation silhouetted against an overcast sky. The two wooded banks draw together toward a pale, indistinct strip at the center of the frame — a sandbar or the far shoreline where the passage narrows — giving the photograph its documentary interest as a record of the Angostura itself rather than of any built structure. A dark shape, possibly a partially submerged rock or post, breaks the water's surface near the right bank, and a triangular shadow at the lower left corner suggests the photographer's own boat or a nearby structure framing the shot. The print is captioned by hand, "Angostura vista desde Chacarita," and carries the archival number 177 in the upper corner alongside an embossed seal.

No bridge or trackwork appears in this frame; the photograph documents the waterway itself rather than railway infrastructure, most likely taken during the same 1923 excursion that produced this catalogue's other Puntarenas-approach material. The file number on this print corresponds to the Archivo Nacional de Costa Rica's own cataloguing for related Ferrocarril al Pacífico photography from the Puntarenas approach, though the specific accession for this plate has not been independently confirmed.

Chacarita and the Angostura: The Last Narrows Before Port

Chacarita sits at the point where the Pacific line's final approach to Puntarenas had to pass through La Angostura — literally "the narrows" — the tightest stretch of the estuary system separating the city's sand-spit peninsula from the mainland. The name has stuck to the place ever since: it appears today as the urban district immediately east of Puntarenas proper, and the narrow passage itself, where coast and estero pinch together, remains a recognizable landmark on the modern Interamerican highway as it was on the railway a century earlier. A 2021–2022 highway-widening project explicitly divided its work into two zones for this reason — "an urban section, that of Chacarita, and another section, properly La Angostura, where we have one margin of coast and another of estero" — the same geography this photograph records from the rail-era vantage point.

Station No. 56 on the numbered line, Chacarita sat at km 110.0, between Carrizal (km 108.0) and Cocal (km 114.0), with the Puntarenas terminus itself just six kilometers further on at km 116.0. Unlike Orotina, Atenas, or San Antonio de Belén, Chacarita carries no Patrimonio Histórico-Arquitectónico designation and no surviving station building forms the centerpiece of local memory; its significance here is geographic rather than architectural, the photograph capturing the narrow water passage the line's builders had to thread, rather than any built structure.

The Angostura's narrowness made it consequential in a later, far darker chapter of regional history unrelated to the railway — the 1975 highway-bus tragedy that claimed fifty lives at this same chokepoint — a tragedy still observed annually in Puntarenas and well outside the documentary scope of this 1923 plate. What this photograph instead preserves is the place before that history: a quiet estuary view, taken on the same kind of excursion that produced this catalogue's other 1923 Puntarenas-approach material, at a narrows that has remained one of the defining features of the approach to Puntarenas across rail, road, and a full century of change.
