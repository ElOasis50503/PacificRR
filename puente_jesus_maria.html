JMB-01 16 January 1923 · Plate 70

Aproximación al Puente — Casa del Guarda

Approach to the Jesús María bridge · trackwalker’s house · plate 70 · 16 January 1923

The date 1/16/23 is inscribed at the upper right and the plate number 70 at the lower left — making this the previously missing plate between MAT-01 (Corte Matamoros, plate 69, same day) and JMB-02 below (bridge, plate 71, same day). All three plates were made on 16 January 1923, tracing the sequence: rock cut at km 83 → trackwalker’s house on the bridge approach → the bridge itself. The original catalogue description confirms this is taken “before arriving at the bridge over the Río Jesús María” and identifies the structure as the casa del guarda.

The photograph looks south along the single track, which curves gently to the right as it approaches the bridge. On the right side of the track, against a cut rock face that rises steeply — the tail end of the Corte Matamoros section or a separate minor cut in the same volcanic terrain — stands a small elevated timber building on timber pile foundations. This is the trackwalker’s house: a two-room or single-room structure with horizontal lap-siding, a single visible window, a simple gabled roof, and a side elevation that suggests a modest lean-to or shed extension. The building is raised off the ground on short piles, typical of tropical railway housing built to avoid ground moisture and provide ventilation.

The guarda (literally “guard” but used here in the sense of trackwalker or guarda-vía) was a railway employee stationed at or near significant infrastructure features — bridges, tunnels, level crossings — to monitor the line, flag trains, and perform daily maintenance walks. Living in a company-provided house alongside the track was standard practice. The location here, on the approach to a steel truss bridge over a river in rugged terrain, is exactly where such a post would be placed: the bridge required daily inspection and any failure of the approach embankment or the structure itself needed immediate reporting.

The vegetation on both sides of the track is dense and dry-season leafless on the left, but the right side shows the moister microclimate of the rock-face shadow: there is more persistent green growth in the cut's angle. The track bed itself is sandy and unballasted in the foreground, transitioning to more solid ground as the curve opens toward the bridge.

JMB-02 16 January 1923 · Plate 71

Puente de Jesús María

Railway bridge over the Río Jesús María · plate 71 · 16 January 1923

Plate 71, taken immediately after JMB-01 on the same survey day, documents the bridge itself from the southern abutment. The single-track through-truss steel bridge is photographed looking north along the deck, its lattice portal frame and X-bracing visible at the far end of the span. The transition from the rock-ballasted earth approach to the open timber deck of the bridge structure is clearly legible in the foreground. Large-leaved vegetation — possibly musaceous species — crowds the right abutment.

Together, plates 70 and 71 document the complete approach-and-bridge sequence in a single survey visit: the trackwalker’s house at the curve below the rock cut, then the bridge deck itself. The pairing is consistent with the survey methodology visible elsewhere in this collection — photographing not just the major structure but its operational context: the dwelling that indicated permanent human presence, the maintained approach, the vegetation that had to be managed to protect the embankment.
