Km 14.4 — station No. 5 on the Pacific line, declared Patrimonio Histórico-Arquitectónico in 2007 and today an active Tren Interurbano stop, photographed here as a working freight and passenger depot in January 1923.

About this spoke: San Antonio de Belén is one of the Pacific line's heritage-protected stations, declared Patrimonio Histórico-Arquitectónico by Decree No. 33637-C (La Gaceta No. 54, 16 March 2007). Built roughly between 1897 and 1902 in a Victorian-inspired, almost entirely wooden idiom, it was the first station on the line equipped to handle both passengers and freight, a dual role that shaped its layout and the community that grew up around it. This spoke gathers a single dated plate of the station yard, inscribed "San Antonio, 1/26/23" (26 January 1923), showing the wooden depot, a substantial masonry water tower, and a long platform building with several figures and a handcart at work on the siding.

The Station, 1923

SANANTBEL-001 26 January 1923

San Antonio, 1923

Station yard, water tower, and platform building

A low wooden depot with a long covered platform stands at center, its corrugated roof sheltering a loading dock where several figures and a handcart are at work beside the track. Immediately behind and to the right rises a tall masonry water tower in two stages — a coursed-block base supporting a dark, banded steel tank — one of the structures that let the line draw on the Ojo de Agua springs to supply water along the route. A long, light-colored building with a row of doors and windows continues the streetfront to the right, while a line of lower wooden structures recedes into the distance on the left, following the track past a tall pole and scattered roadside vegetation. The print is inscribed by hand, "San Antonio, 1/26/23" (26 January 1923).

The Archivo Nacional de Costa Rica catalogues a related set of San Antonio de Belén station photographs from 1923 under reference CR-AN-AH-FO-005939, the same number carried by this print, suggesting this plate belongs to that same documented series. Built roughly between 1897 and 1902 in a wooden, Victorian-inflected style, the station was the first on the Pacific line designed to handle passengers and freight together — a dual role reflected here in the platform's loading dock standing alongside what would have been the building's passenger-facing rooms.

San Antonio de Belén: The First Mixed Station

San Antonio de Belén holds a particular place in the Pacific line's early history: period sources describe it as the first station on the route built to handle both passengers and freight under one roof, a combination that shaped the building's footprint and, in time, the working life of the community around it. Construction is generally dated to between 1897 and 1902, in a wooden, Victorian-inflected style typical of the line's early stations; the rails themselves reached Belén in mid-May 1900, the arrival announced by the locomotive María Cecilia, with the station's first formal agency entering service around 1902 — a few years ahead of the line's full 1910 inauguration under president Ricardo Jiménez Oreamuno.

Three employees ran the station day to day: an agent who handled communication with the Pacific Railway's central offices, a warehouseman responsible for the goods stored on site, and a stevedore who watched over the loaded sacks and cargo, checking coffee shipments in particular against the manifests their owners declared. Through their hands passed coffee bound for the European market, along with peanuts, rice, beans, and avocados arriving from Orotina, Quebradas, and Hacienda Vieja, sand and stone for track maintenance, and cattle shipped down from Guanacaste ranches — some of it from the herds of the cattleman Julio Sánchez. For the wider community, the station was equally a gateway to leisure: families travelling to Puntarenas for the first time recalled it as a singular adventure, complete with vendors moving through the cars selling gallos de papa and refrescos.

The line's 1930 electrification gave San Antonio de Belén's water tower — the structure rising beside the depot in this plate — a renewed purpose: drawing on the springs at Ojo de Agua, it helped supply water not only to passing locomotives but to Puntarenas and other communities along the route, a role formalized with the opening of a public bathing facility, owned by the Ferrocarril al Pacífico, on 8 December 1937. The station was declared Patrimonio Histórico-Arquitectónico by Decree No. 33637-C, published in La Gaceta No. 54 of 16 March 2007, and today stands rehabilitated as one of two stations on the line — alongside the Pacific terminal in San José — serving active Tren Interurbano passenger trains, carrying the station's century-old mixed-use role into the present.
