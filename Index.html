Fifty-eight numbered stations once carried coffee from the Central Valley down to the Pacific port of Puntarenas — a line built in fits and starts across nearly four decades, electrified in 1930, and finally closed in 1995, leaving behind the steel bridge that appears in two of the collection's Paynter Bros. postcards.

Companion volume: this supplement covers the Ferrocarril al Pacífico (San José–Puntarenas), the sister line to the Atlantic Railway covered separately in Estaciones del Ferrocarril al Atlántico. Its anchor crossing, Puente Río Grande, is also catalogued as T-04 in Transport Costa Rica Histórica (v2.7); the Río Grande spoke here gathers both Paynter Bros. postcards plus a 1922 National Geographic plate, with full engineering history.

The Line — San José to Puntarenas

Unlike the Atlantic line, the Pacific Railway's own station boards carried sequential numbers, and FECOSA's archive preserves the full run — 58 stations, distances measured from San José this time, the opposite convention from the Atlantic line. A short branch off Ciruelas (No. 10) served Alajuela by way of Lagunillas and Montecillos, numbered 11–13 in the same sequence; a separate source consulted for the Ciruelas spoke names the same general stretch as passing through "Coyol de Alajuela" rather than Lagunillas — possibly an alternate or more specific name for the same locality, not yet reconciled here.

Puente Río Grande — km 36.8

STA-P01 Now fully illustrated · see the dedicated spoke

Puente Río Grande

Between Cebadilla (No. 18) and Concepción de Atenas (No. 19) — 36.8 km from San José

The single most photographed structure on the Pacific line in this collection: a Paynter Bros. construction-phase postcard showing the cantilevered erection arm and cableway-suspended work basket used to build the crossing before any deck existed, a second Paynter Bros. card of the completed lattice-truss deck bridge carrying a full mixed train, and a 1922 National Geographic Magazine plate that used the same completed bridge as unrelated picture-desk illustration. All three plates, together with the bridge's full engineering history — designer Theodore Cooper, fabricator Milliken Bros., completed March 1902, and its 2002 Patrimonio Histórico-Arquitectónico listing — are gathered in the dedicated Puente Río Grande spoke.

Heritage Stations

STA-P02 Designated 2021, La Nación / state decree

Five Stations Declared Patrimonio Histórico-Arquitectónico

San José, Atenas, Escobal, Quebradas, Orotina

Of the line's 58 numbered stations, six carry formal Costa Rican architectural-heritage protection: San José (No. 1, the Pacific terminal, rehabilitated as an active Tren Interurbano stop), San Antonio de Belén (No. 5, declared Patrimonio Histórico-Arquitectónico by Decree No. 33637-C in 2007 and likewise an active Interurbano stop), Atenas (No. 19, now the Museo Ferroviario de Atenas), Escobal (No. 23), Quebradas (No. 24), and Orotina (No. 29, today an agricultural-supply warehouse, where a 2017 community restoration effort relocated a Siemens electric locomotive, No. 130, for cosmetic restoration and display).

The Puntarenas terminal itself — the line's original raison d'être, reached in 1910 — survives in a different form: the old customs house at the port is now occupied by the Universidad Técnica Nacional's Pacific campus.

Historical Note

Building the Line, 1857–1930

The Pacific line took far longer to reach a working state than its Caribbean counterpart, and for a more prosaic reason than the Atlantic line's landslide-prone terrain: money kept running out. An 1857 contract with the English entrepreneur Richard Farrer produced only a short mule-drawn tramway between Puntarenas and Tivives — a "burrocarril" that lasted barely a year. Minor Keith's Northern Railway Company opened a Puntarenas–Esparza branch in 1882, using equipment intended for the Atlantic project, but a true trunk line connecting San José to the Pacific coast did not begin in earnest until president Rafael Iglesias Castro signed a contract with William Lynn in 1895.

Lynn's construction ran continuously from September 1897 until 1903, when he announced he had exhausted his financing. The government of Ascensión Esquivel Ibarra then completed the work using national funds and labour — including, at Orotina, the hand-dug Paso de Matamoros, cut through solid rock with dynamite in 1909. A final 1908 contract with the American engineer Warren H. Knowlton finished the remaining stretch from Cascajal to El Roble, where it joined the older 1882 Esparza branch in July 1910, completing the line's full 132 kilometres between Puntarenas and San José — see the Empalme spoke for a separate, undated photograph of a rail junction elsewhere on the network. The line was electrified in 1930, using equipment from the German firm AEG and power from a small hydroelectric plant built at Tacares — making it one of the first electrified railways in a tropical country.

Passenger and freight service on the Pacific line ended in 1995, the same year that closed the Atlantic line, when Incofer was shut down by government decree. Periodic tourist excursions to Puntarenas ran from the old Pacific station in the early 2000s, and as of 2019 Incofer has studied reconstructing sections of the route — including a proposed tunnel near Ruta 27 to bypass the same kind of landslide-prone ground that doomed long stretches of the Atlantic line.
