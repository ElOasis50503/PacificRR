Km 11.5 — station No. 4 on the Pacific line, in the district of La Uruca, named for the Planta Hidroeléctrica Electriona that the Compañía Nacional Hidroeléctrica built nearby on the Río Virilla, completed in August 1928.

About this spoke: Electriona is an unusual case among this catalogue's stations: a stop named not for the town or district it served, but for a company. The Compañía Nacional Hidroeléctrica, formed in 1922, built its Planta Electriona on the Río Virilla at La Uruca, completing it in August 1928; the name stuck closely enough to the surrounding area that "Electriona" survives today as one of La Uruca's official barrios, and evidently gave this railway stop its name as well. The first plate in this spoke shows the platform shed itself, lettered "F.C.AL.P" along its roofline — Ferrocarril al Pacífico spelled out in full, rather than abbreviated. A second plate, showing road construction elsewhere in the same district, is grouped here on geography alone; it documents La Uruca's road infrastructure rather than the railway, and is presented as a separate subject rather than as part of the station's own history.

Estación Electriona

The platform shed, lettered "F.C.AL.P"

A deep-roofed platform shed frames the view down the track, its peak lettered "F.C.AL.P" — Ferrocarril al Pacífico, spelled out across the gable rather than abbreviated to initials as on most of this catalogue's other station signage. A passenger coach stands under the canopy at left, and along the open-sided platform to the right, a small group of station staff and travelers in period dress are posed for the camera, several seated on what appears to be a weighing scale or baggage cart. In the foreground, a man operates a tripod-mounted camera or surveying instrument, his back to the viewer, suggesting this photograph was itself taken during a documentary survey of the line rather than as a casual snapshot.

No explicit caption survives identifying this as Electriona, but the image is catalogued under that name and the architecture — a single long platform shed rather than a full enclosed station building — is consistent with a modest suburban stop close to the capital rather than a major junction or terminus.

The district's name in this period was tied to the hydroelectric company and its plant on the Río Virilla; this photograph instead shows the area's ordinary road infrastructure being improved, a reminder that the railway was never the only public works project running through the same small stretch of countryside outside San José.

Electriona: A Station Named for a Power Plant

Most stations on the Pacific line took their names from the towns, districts, or landowning families along the route. Electriona is a clear exception — and an unusually traceable one. In 1922, a group of Costa Rican investors led by Enrique Ortiz Rivera formed the Compañía Nacional Hidroeléctrica, S.A., intending to compete with the existing electric utilities already serving San José. Their flagship project was a new hydroelectric plant on the Río Virilla at La Uruca, a few kilometres outside the capital — the same river documented elsewhere in this catalogue at its railway crossing near Pavas. Land and property complications delayed construction, and by the time the plant finally entered service on the morning of 5 August 1928, the Compañía Nacional Hidroeléctrica itself had already been acquired by the American utility holding company EBASCO (Electric Bond and Share). The plant kept its name regardless: Planta Hidroeléctrica Electriona, reported variously at 2,720 kW total capacity or as two units of 1,550 kW each.

The plant's name spread to the surrounding area closely enough that "Electriona" is today one of the twenty-three official barrios of La Uruca, San José's seventh district — alongside neighbors with names like Cristal, Marimil, and Vuelta del Virilla. The railway station that grew up nearby on the Pacific line, station No. 4 at km 11.5, took the same name, becoming one of the few stops on the entire network named for industrial infrastructure rather than for a settlement, a family, or a natural feature. By the time Electriona the plant passed into the Compañía Nacional de Fuerza y Luz in 1941 — the merger that also absorbed Minor Keith's old Costa Rica Electric Light and Traction Company — the station's name had already long outlived any direct operational connection between the two; it simply stayed, a small linguistic fossil of a 1920s corporate venture preserved in a railway timetable and, eventually, on modern San José's district maps.

The second plate in this spoke belongs to a different, unrelated project in the same general area: a wheeled traction engine hauling fill along a deep cut as part of ordinary road-building in La Uruca, captioned simply "Haciendo caminos." It is included here for geographic convenience rather than historical connection — there is no indication that this roadwork served the railway, the power plant, or any single coordinated infrastructure push, and it should be read as a separate document of district-level public works rather than as part of Electriona's own story.
