CAS-01 19 January 1923 · Plate 67

Cascajal — Alzando Leña

Loading firewood · plate 67 · 19 January 1923

Caption inscribed: Cascajal. Alzando leña. 1/19/23; plate number 67 at lower right. The photograph shows a long flatcar on the left track piled high with rough-cut roundwood and split timber — workers mid-task, some carrying or stacking. A pig crosses the right track. At right, a substantial multi-storey industrial structure dominates, possibly a lumber mill or beneficio serving the lowland zone.

The date is 19 January 1923, three days after the Corte Matamoros and Puente Jesús María plates (16 January) and two days before this collection’s earliest documented date at Escobal (24 January). The firewood being loaded almost certainly served as locomotive fuel, staged at Cascajal for replenishment. See also CAS-02 below, which documents active siding construction at the same station 3.5 months later.

CAS-02 3 May 1923

Extensión Apartadero de Cascajal

Siding extension under construction · 3 May 1923

Caption reads Extensión Apartadero de Cascajal. Mayo 3, 1923. No plate number is visible. An apartadero is a passing loop or siding — a short section of secondary track that allows trains moving in opposite directions to pass each other on a single-track line. The extensión (extension) of the Cascajal apartadero was an active civil works project on 3 May 1923, photographed here for the survey record.

The photograph shows the construction in progress, looking along the line of the new siding toward where it will rejoin the main track. In the middle ground, a work gang of five or six labourers in broad-brimmed hats stands along the partly-laid track, their tools and posture suggesting a momentary pause during heavy manual work — possibly tamping ballast, aligning sleepers, or preparing the subgrade. To the right of the workers, a small flat spoil wagon or skip sits on the newly laid track, used to move excavated material away from the construction face. The surrounding terrain is heavily disturbed: the embankment sides show fresh earth cuts, and the vegetation is completely cleared within the working zone.

This photograph is notable in the survey collection as the clearest documentation of active railway construction or infrastructure improvement in progress, as distinct from operational survey views. The Cascajal apartadero extension in May 1923 represents the Pacific Railway actively investing in capacity improvements while simultaneously being photographed for a comprehensive survey — consistent with the government’s pending acquisition of the line from private operators and the need to understand exactly what infrastructure was being taken on.

Placed in sequence alongside CAS-01 (19 January 1923, firewood loading at the same station), these two plates together document Cascajal across a 3.5-month span: first in active freight operations, then as a construction site. The siding extension visible here would have increased the station’s capacity to hold trains while others passed — particularly relevant for the lumber and agricultural freight traffic evidenced by CAS-01 and the Orotina plates.
