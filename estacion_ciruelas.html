Km 22.7 — station No. 10 on the Pacific line, 8 km short of Alajuela centro by the branch that opened here in 1906, after years of political effort by diputados Julio Acosta and Francisco Saborío to secure the connection.

About this spoke: Ciruelas was a junction station — the point where the Ramal Alajuela branched off the main Pacific line toward Coyol de Alajuela, Montecillos, and Alajuela centro, a connection secured after years of political effort by diputados Julio Acosta and Francisco Saborío and opened for regular service in 1906, four years before the main trunk line itself was completed. This spoke gathers five plates: three documenting the main-line station and its bridge across two visits in December 1922 and January–May 1923; a fourth, captioned "Sobre el Ciruelas — Alajuela," showing a different bridge specifically on the Ramal Alajuela branch; and a fifth, undated and uncaptioned beyond the word "Foto," showing a masonry road-arch bridge framing a steel railway truss bridge over the same river — included here provisionally on the strength of its proximity to the fourth plate in the source numbering, but with its location and identification explicitly unconfirmed.

The Station and Bridge

CIRUELAS-001 December 1922

Puente Ciruelas

Broadside view from below the trestle

A steel trestle bridge crosses a wooded cutting on a shallow upward grade, its riveted lattice deck carried on a run of braced steel towers stepping down the slope toward a masonry pier at right. Dense forest crowds both sides of the cutting below, with a dirt path running along the base of the embankment. The print is captioned by hand, "Puente Cirvelas" (a period spelling of Ciruelas), dated December 1922.

CIRUELAS-002 20 January 1923

Ciruelas

The station building, switch, and yard

A wide-eaved station building with a conical, thatch-like roof stands at the center of the frame, two figures visible on its small covered porch; a flagpole rises beside it. The main line curves in from the right while a second track diverges left through a hand-thrown switch in the foreground — the junction point for the Ramal Alajuela, branching off toward Coyol and Montecillos. A second, smaller building of similar style stands further back at left, and bare-limbed trees frame the yard on both sides. The print is captioned "Ciruelas, 1/20/23" with the number 22 in the corner.

This is the junction itself: the switch visible in the foreground is what let trains continuing on the main line toward Atenas and Puntarenas separate from those branching off toward Alajuela centro — a connection the province had pursued for over a decade before the Ramal Alajuela finally opened for regular service in 1906.

CIRUELAS-003 6 May 1923

Puente de Ciruelas

Looking straight down the deck, five months after CIRUELAS-001

A companion view to the first plate in this spoke, taken from the deck itself looking straight down the bridge's length: two parallel rails run the full visible distance toward a building at the far end, flanked by a second, disused-looking rail at left and dense scrub on both sides of the right-of-way. Unlike most single-track crossings catalogued elsewhere in this collection, this deck appears to carry two tracks side by side — possibly reflecting a siding or passing point at this junction station, consistent with Ciruelas's role as the point where the Ramal Alajuela diverged from the main line. The print is captioned "Puente de Ciruelas," dated 6 May 1923.

CIRUELAS-004 Undated

Sobre el Ciruelas — Alajuela

A different bridge, captioned for the Ramal Alajuela branch

Four young men sit and stand along the rail of a timber-decked steel trestle bridge, photographed from below and to one side. Three sit with legs dangling over the structure's edge while a fourth stands at the far right, one arm raised holding a hat or bottle toward the camera. The bridge's braced steel towers step down in a repeating pattern typical of this collection's other Pacific-line crossings, with open countryside and a line of trees visible through the structure. A printed caption at lower right reads "SOBRE EL CIRUELAS — ALAJUELA."

The "ALAJUELA" designation in the printed caption places this crossing specifically on the Ramal Alajuela — the branch line that left the main Pacific trunk at Ciruelas station and ran via Coyol and Montecillos to Alajuela centro — rather than the main-line Puente de Ciruelas documented in the two bridge plates above. Whether this is a second bridge over the same Río Ciruelas, downstream or upstream of the station, or a crossing of a different watercourse further along the branch toward Alajuela, is not resolved by the print itself; what the caption does establish clearly is that it belongs to the branch rather than the trunk line.

CIRUELAS-005 Undated · location unconfirmed

Two Bridges, One River

Flagged as uncertain — included here provisionally, not a confirmed Ciruelas plate

A substantial brick masonry road-bridge arch fills the foreground, its coursed brickwork rising to a parapet where two figures stand at opposite ends; through the arch's opening, a steel lattice-truss railway bridge crosses the same river further upstream, its diagonal cross-bracing clearly visible against the sky. Below, a small group of adults and children stand on rocks at the water's edge beneath the masonry arch, with dense riverside vegetation framing the scene on both banks. The only printed text on the plate is the word "Foto" in the lower corner — no caption, date, or location is given.

This plate is included in the Ciruelas spoke provisionally, on the basis of its physical proximity in the source numbering sequence to the confirmed "Sobre el Ciruelas — Alajuela" plate above, and because the composition — an older masonry road crossing paired with a later steel railway bridge over the same river — is consistent with several documented double-crossings along the Alajuela corridor, including the historic 1840s masonry arch bridge over the Río Virilla on the old San José–Alajuela cart road, situated near the railway's own later crossing of that river. However, no source consulted for this catalogue confirms that this specific photograph shows the Virilla, the Ciruelas, or another river entirely, and the location should be treated as unresolved until a captioned or otherwise identifiable print surfaces.

Ciruelas: Where the Alajuela Branch Began

The toponym "Ciruelas" predates the railway by more than two centuries — the name comes from a native fruit tree of the Tropical Dry Forest, with documented references to the place going back to notarial protocols of 1 December 1662 and 23 April 1672, long before any rail line crossed this stretch of the Central Valley's western edge. By the early twentieth century, Ciruelas had become something more specific: the junction point where a branch line split off the Pacific trunk route toward Alajuela, the province's own provincial capital, which the main line itself did not serve directly.

Securing that branch took sustained political effort. The 1895 contract between president Rafael Iglesias Castro and the entrepreneur William Lynn set the main Pacific line in motion, but a connection to Alajuela required separate advocacy — credited in local histories to diputados Julio Acosta and Francisco Saborío, whose efforts helped establish the case for what became the Ramal Alajuela. Surveying work followed in 1903, with engineer Leoncio Martínez's plan confirming the terrain was suitable for a station at Ciruelas, and the branch finally opened for regular freight and passenger service in 1906 — four years ahead of the main trunk line's own full completion in 1910. From this station, the branch ran 8 kilometers through the industrial zone of Coyol de Alajuela and the district of Montecillos before reaching Molinos de Costa Rica and Alajuela centro itself, where the line's terminal building still stands near the city's modern bus terminal.

The first three plates in this spoke, photographed across a five-month span from December 1922 to May 1923, capture the main-line junction from three angles: the bridge from below, the station and its switch from the platform, and the bridge deck itself looking down its length, where two parallel tracks suggest the kind of siding or passing arrangement a junction station would need to sort main-line and branch-line traffic. A fourth plate moves onto the branch itself, its printed "ALAJUELA" caption confirming that at least one further bridge crossing existed somewhere along the Ramal Alajuela's route toward the provincial capital, separate from the main-line crossing documented above. Together, the four confirmed plates document a station whose importance came not from its own size — Ciruelas itself was a modest stop — but from the connection it carried: the only rail link the provincial capital of Alajuela had to the Pacific port that gave the whole line its purpose.

A fifth plate, showing a masonry road-arch bridge framing a steel railway truss crossing of the same river, is grouped with this spoke provisionally rather than confidently. Its only printed text is the word "Foto," with no caption, date, or place name to anchor it, and while the broader Alajuela corridor is known to have at least one historic double-crossing of this general kind — the 1840s masonry arch carrying the old San José–Alajuela cart road, built near the railway's later crossing of the Río Virilla — this catalogue has not confirmed that the plate shown here depicts that crossing, the Ciruelas itself, or a different river along the same branch. It is presented honestly as an unresolved attribution, included here on the strength of its place in the source numbering sequence rather than on any caption of its own.
