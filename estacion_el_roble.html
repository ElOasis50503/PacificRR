Km 106.0 — station No. 53 on the Pacific line, where Warren H. Knowlton's new construction from Cascajal met the older Puntarenas–Esparza line in July 1910, finally completing the route between San José and the Pacific port.

About this spoke: El Roble was the literal joining point of two railways built decades apart. The original Puntarenas–Esparza branch had operated since 1882 under Minor Keith's Northern Railway Company; the new San José trunk line, contracted out in its final stage to Warren H. Knowlton in 1908, was built outward from Cascajal to meet that older line here. The connection opened in July 1910 as part of a final push of openings — Cascajal–Salinas, Salinas–Fertica, and Fertica–El Roble — that completed the 132-kilometre route between Puntarenas and San José. Like several other stations on this stretch (Barranca, Salinas, Coyolar, Quebradas, Montecillos), El Roble was equipped with a toril, a livestock loading pen, for moving cattle on and off the line's stockcars.

El Roble

Station, water tower, and toril at the line's junction point

A long, low station building with a deep overhanging roof stands beside a curving single track, its open-sided platform supported on masonry piers along the trackside edge. A water tower rises among the trees just beyond the station, and further along the same building line, a smaller covered structure with its own low roof — consistent with a toril, the livestock loading pen this station was known to have — stands beside what appear to be stockcars and other rolling stock partly obscured by trees at the right of the frame. The print is numbered 102 in the lower left corner; the original archival title identifies this as the station and right-of-way at El Roble de Puntarenas, where the lines coming from Esparta and from San José met.

No single rail line is visible splitting into two in this particular frame, but the photograph's documented subject — the empalme of the lines bound for Esparta and for San José — places it squarely at the point where Costa Rica's two differently aged Pacific-line construction efforts physically joined: the old 1882 Northern Railway branch to Esparza, and the newer trunk line built south from Cascajal under Warren H. Knowlton's 1908 contract.

El Roble: Where the Two Lines Finally Joined

For nearly three decades, the line that would eventually carry coffee all the way from San José to Puntarenas existed as two unconnected pieces. The older piece, a branch from Puntarenas to Esparza, opened in 1882 under Minor Keith's Northern Railway Company, using equipment originally intended for the Atlantic project. The newer piece — the actual San José trunk line, contracted to William Lynn in 1895 and continued under national funds after his 1903 default — crept toward the coast across the following decade and a half, through Cebadilla, Atenas, Orotina, and the difficult low-lying ground around Barranca and Boca Barranca documented elsewhere in this catalogue. In 1904 the government settled definitively on Puntarenas, rather than the older terminal at Tivives, as the line's final destination. In 1908 a new contract went to the American engineer Warren H. Knowlton, who committed to building the remaining stretch from Cascajal to El Roble specifically in order to join it to the existing Esparza–Puntarenas line.

Knowlton's section opened in stages in July 1910 — Cascajal to Salinas, Salinas to Fertica, and finally Fertica to El Roble, along with branch connections to Purruja and from Ciruelas to Molinos — bringing the through route between Puntarenas and San José to its full 132 kilometres on 23 July 1910. El Roble was the literal seam in that join: the point where two railways built eight years apart by two different concessionaires under two different presidents finally became one continuous line. Like several of its neighboring stations along this stretch — Barranca, Salinas, Coyolar, Quebradas, and Montecillos — El Roble was also equipped with a toril, a dedicated loading pen for cattle being moved on and off the line's specialized stockcars, a detail that speaks to the cattle trade running alongside coffee as one of the corridor's working freight businesses.

Steam power served the line at first; electrification followed in 1930, after a 1926 contract with the German firm AEG to build a hydroelectric plant at Tacares specifically to supply the new system. By the time passenger and freight service ended across the whole network in 1995, El Roble had spent eight decades as one of the quieter but structurally essential stations on the route — not a terminus, not a major town stop, but the point that had made through travel between Costa Rica's capital and its Pacific port possible in the first place.
