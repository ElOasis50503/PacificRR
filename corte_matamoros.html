MAT-01 16 January 1923

Corte Matamoros — km 83

Rock cut · between Uvita (No. 38, km 82.0) and Jesús María (No. 39, km 85.9) · Puntarenas Province

The caption inscribed at the top of the plate reads Corte Matamoros, km 83. 1/16/23, and the plate number 69 at lower right places this image three days before the Cascajal photograph (plate 67, 19 January 1923) and three days after the unknown plate 66 or earlier — the survey was evidently moving south along the line toward Puntarenas in mid-January 1923. A corte is a road or railway cut: an excavation through rising ground to maintain a level grade, the alternative to a tunnel or a steep curve. This one passes through solid rock.

The photograph is taken from track level looking northwest (toward San José), down the single-track line as it threads a narrow passage between near-vertical walls of dark volcanic rock. The walls rise perhaps 8–10 metres above rail level and are only a few metres apart at the base — barely wide enough for the rolling stock clearance gauge. Vegetation has colonized the upper ledges and the cut walls wherever soil has gathered, producing a dramatic overgrown canyon effect. No workers or equipment are visible; the line is in active service.

The Paso de Matamoros was one of the most celebrated engineering feats of the Pacific Railway construction. According to the construction history in this catalogue, the cut was made “by hand” using dynamite in 1909, during the period when the government of Ascensión Esquivel Ibarra was completing the line from its own resources after contractor William Lynn’s financing collapsed in 1903. The km 83 designation places it approximately 800 metres south of Uvita station (km 82.0) and roughly 2.9 km north of Jesús María station (km 85.9). The hub note for Uvita (“Paso Matamoros follows, 83.9 km”) recorded km 83.9; the photograph caption says km 83 — this small discrepancy may reflect different measurement conventions, or the “83.9” may describe the southern exit of a longer cut.

The dry-season foliage and the January date are consistent with the broader survey sequence. The steep rock faces and sparse vegetation on the upper ledges are characteristic of the transitional dry-forest zone of the Pacific lowland approach, at an elevation where the line has descended well below the Central Valley escarpment but has not yet reached the flat coastal plain.
