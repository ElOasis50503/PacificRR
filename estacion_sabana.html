The Line at Sabana

SABANA-001 c. 1900–1915 (estimated)

Sabana

The line curving through open pasture, with a horse-drawn carriage on the adjoining road

A single track curves through open grassland in the foreground, running on a low ballast bed alongside an unpaved road where a horse-drawn carriage, carrying what appear to be two or three passengers, makes its way past a scattering of utility poles. The poles themselves carry an unusually dense fan of overhead wires — telegraph or telephone lines rather than the electric traction wire the line would eventually carry after 1930 — strung out toward a vanishing point at left. A line of low buildings and scattered trees marks the middle distance, with the Central Valley's surrounding mountains rising hazily behind. A single mature tree stands at right, its canopy framing the upper edge of the photograph. The print is captioned simply "Sabana."

No deck bridge, station building, or signage is visible in the frame — the photograph documents the open, still-rural character of La Sabana in this period, when the area was the city's parade ground and pasture rather than the park or, decades later, the airport it would become. The absence of any visible electrification hardware suggests a date before the line's 1930 electrification, consistent with the overall character of the image as an early-period view, though no inscribed date survives to confirm this more precisely.

Sabana: The City's First Mile Outside the Terminus

Station No. 2, at km 2.7, was the first stop the Pacific line made after leaving its San José terminus — a short hop across what was then the open grazing land and parade ground of La Sabana, well outside the built-up city of this period. The contrast with La Sabana's later history is stark: across the twentieth century the same ground would serve in turn as the country's principal airfield (the subject of a separate San José aviation postcard thread in this archive), and eventually as the Parque Metropolitano La Sabana that occupies the site today. In this photograph, none of that lies ahead yet; the land is simple pasture, crossed by a single rail line and an unpaved road, with telegraph or telephone wiring strung along wooden poles overhead.

The absence of any visible electrification equipment is the most useful dating clue available: the line was electrified in 1930, using equipment from the German firm AEG and power drawn from a small hydroelectric plant at Tacares, and any photograph from after that date would very likely show the overhead traction wire and supporting structure that came with it. Combined with the exclusively horse-drawn road traffic visible here, this plate most plausibly dates from the years around or shortly after the line's full 1910 completion under president Ricardo Jiménez Oreamuno — a modest, unglamorous record of the railway's first short stretch out of the capital, taken at a point on the route that would go on to have a far more eventful twentieth century than the track itself.
