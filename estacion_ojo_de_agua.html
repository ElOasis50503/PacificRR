Km 18.7 — station No. 7 on the Pacific line, among the route's earliest stations, serving the balneario at Ojo de Agua that for decades drew its visitors almost entirely by rail from San José.

About this spoke: Ojo de Agua was among the line's earliest stations, built in the same first-decade-of-the-1900s wave as San José, Pavas, Nuestro Amo, Turrucares, and Hacienda Vieja. But the station's later importance came from something built decades after the line itself: the balneario, a public swimming resort fed by a natural spring from the Barva aquifer. Sources consulted for this catalogue disagree on the balneario's opening date — one account dates it to the third presidential term of Ricardo Jiménez Oreamuno (1932–1936); another, drawing on direct testimony from the son of the engineer who built it, places its formal construction in February–December 1952, commissioned by the Ferrocarril's own administration; a third source gives 1937. This catalogue presents all three rather than silently choosing one.

Ojo de Agua: The Railway's Own Resort

The station itself dates to the line's earliest construction, built in the same first decade of the twentieth century as San José, Pavas, Nuestro Amo, Turrucares, and Hacienda Vieja. For its first three decades, Ojo de Agua was simply one stop among many — named, like the balneario that would later make it famous, for the natural spring (the "eye of water") that emerges from the Barva aquifer nearby. The earliest informal use of the spring had nothing to do with leisure: in the late 1920s and early 1930s, a pipeline was built from the spring to supply drinking water to Puntarenas, more than a hundred kilometres away, and workers building that pipeline piled stones in the streambed to form a small pool where they bathed after each day's labor.

What turned that informal work-crew pool into Costa Rica's most enduring public balneario is documented with real specificity, though the sources consulted for this catalogue do not agree on exactly when it happened. The most detailed account, drawn from family testimony, states that in February 1952 the Ferrocarril's own administration commissioned engineer Francisco Bolaños Varela to build the balneario; working without heavy machinery, using dynamite, picks, and shovels, with stone hauled from Cerro Dantas, sand from Caldera, and other materials from San José, a crew of twelve completed the project by December of that same year, opening to the public on 8 December 1952. Other sources place the balneario's origin earlier, during Ricardo Jiménez Oreamuno's third presidential term (1932–1936), or as early as 1937. This catalogue does not resolve the discrepancy; it is possible that informal bathing facilities existed at the site for decades before the 1952 formal construction project that several sources describe in such specific detail, and that different accounts are describing different stages of the same site's long, gradual development rather than contradicting one another outright.

What is not disputed is the resort's dependence on the railway that gave it a station number in the first place. For decades, visitors from San José bought tickets at the Pacific station, rode the train to San Antonio de Belén, and walked the remaining distance to the balneario — a ritual remembered fondly enough that it appears in nearly every account of the site's history. Ojo de Agua remains open today, still fed by the same natural spring, administered now by the Instituto Costarricense de Puertos del Pacífico (INCOP) rather than the long-since-closed Ferrocarril, a rare case in this catalogue of a station's purpose outliving the railway line that built it.
