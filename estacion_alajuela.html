ALJ-00 12 January 1923 · Plate 28

Alajuela — Patio y Cobertizo

Yard view with freight shed and inspection trolley · plate 28 · 12 January 1923

Caption reads Alajuela 1/12/23; plate number 28 at upper right. This is the earliest-dated Alajuela plate in the collection — four and a half months before the station-building view (plate 105, 30 May 1923, documented as ALJ-01 below) and the bodega plate (ALJ-02, same date). The low plate number relative to the January plates documented elsewhere in this catalogue (Quebradas, plate 50, 24 April; Cascajal, plate 67, 19 January) suggests the survey visited the Ramal Alajuela terminus very early in its programme, possibly among its first subjects.

The photograph looks along the multi-track yard from platform level. At left, two substantial buildings front the right-of-way: a two-storey structure with a steep gabled roof and prominent attic vents, and beside it a long single-storey building with lettering partially legible on its end wall (the visible fragment reads roughly ...RICULTURA, suggesting a name connected to agriculture — perhaps a Sociedad de Agricultores or similar institutional building, consistent with Alajuela’s role as a coffee-growing centre). At right, a large open-sided shed with a dramatically pitched, asymmetric roof shelters the platform — its roof structure is exposed timber trusswork, less finished than the station building seen in ALJ-01, suggesting this may be an older or more utilitarian freight-handling structure.

In the foreground, a four-wheeled motorized rail trolley or speeder sits on the nearer track, its boxy engine housing and open frame consistent with early-1920s railway maintenance-of-way equipment used for track inspection. A man in a broad-brimmed hat stands on the platform at right, one hand on the platform edge, watching the camera. The yard shows at least four parallel tracks, indicating Alajuela’s importance as a terminal with substantial siding capacity for a branch line terminus.

ALJ-01 30 May 1923 · Plate 105

Estación Alajuela — Patio y Bodega

Station building, front elevation · plate 105 · 30 May 1923

Caption reads Estación Alajuela Mayo 30, 1923; plate number 105. Four and a half months after ALJ-00 (plate 28, 12 January), this plate documents the station building proper — a single-storey hipped-roof structure with a covered veranda on turned posts, photographed from the front yard with two young ornamental trees planted symmetrically before the entrance. At right, a passenger coach and what appears to be a push-car are visible alongside the platform; the bodega is visible at far right and receives its own plate in ALJ-02 below.

The architectural type — hipped bungalow with broad overhanging eaves — is characteristic of the Pacific Railway’s station family, similar in form to the heritage-listed stations at San José and San Antonio de Belén, though on a smaller scale appropriate to a branch terminus.

ALJ-02 30 May 1923

Bodega Alajuela

Freight warehouse · Ramal Alajuela terminus · 30 May 1923

Caption reads Bodega Alajuela Mayo 30, 1923 — the same date as ALJ-01. This plate shows the freight warehouse briefly visible at the far right edge of ALJ-01: a substantial single-storey masonry structure in two-tone render, served by a raised loading platform with a timber access ramp. A horse-drawn two-wheeled cart stands beside the platform, and a figure in a broad-brimmed hat occupies the open doorway, giving scale to the building’s c. 6–7 m ridge height.

The masonry construction contrasts with the wood-frame station building in ALJ-01 — a common distinction on the Pacific Railway between passenger facilities (timber) and freight warehouses (more fire-resistant masonry).
