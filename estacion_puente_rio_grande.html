Km 36.8 — the single most photographed structure on the entire Pacific line, a 219.38-metre two-hinged deck truss arching some 102–105 metres above the gorge, designed by American engineer Theodore Cooper and completed in March 1902.

About this spoke: Puente Río Grande is the single most photographed structure on the Pacific line within this collection — represented here by both halves of a Paynter Bros. before-and-after pair (construction phase and completed bridge) and, separately, by an unrelated 1922 National Geographic Magazine plate that used the same bridge as wholly independent picture-desk illustration. The bridge sits on the steepest, most engineering-intensive stretch of the entire route — the descent from the Central Valley through Turrúcares and Cebadilla toward Atenas — and would have been one of the principal obstacles facing William Lynn's original 1897 contract and the national-funds completion effort that followed after he ran out of financing in 1903.

The Bridge

PRG-001 Paynter Bros., San José, c. 1900–1910

Puente Río Grande

Completed bridge, with a mixed train crossing

A lattice-truss deck bridge rises on a broad parabolic arch over a deep, forested gorge, the steel members riveted in a dense triangulated pattern between the arch and the deck above. A full mixed train — steam locomotive, tender, a boxcar, and at least one passenger coach — crosses the span, with several figures, evidently railway workers, posed near the engine for scale at the bridge's far end. Captioned simply "Puente" and "Río Grande" in the card's printed corners, with the publisher's imprint, "Paynter Bros.," set in the lower margin.

The bridge's height and the bridge's arch are best appreciated from below: a companion construction-phase card in this spoke shows the same crossing before any deck existed, worked entirely from cableways slung across the open gorge.

PRG-002 Paynter Bros., San José, c. 1900–1902

Puente "Río Grande" en Construcción

Two construction-phase views, before any deck existed

A two-panel souvenir card captioned "Puente 'Rio Grande' en construcción," its images framed by a decorative floral border and the Paynter Bros. imprint along the left edge. The left panel shows a cantilevered steel erection arm projecting out over the gorge from one cliff face, a cableway slung from it down to the unbuilt far side; the right panel, a tighter view of the same cableway, shows a small suspended basket carrying several workmen across the open chasm, the forested slopes of the gorge framing the void beneath them. Neither panel shows any trace of a deck, truss, or rail bed — this is the bridge before it existed, built outward from both cliff faces using exactly the kind of aerial cableway rigging that let crews work a 219-metre span over a 100-metre drop without first building anything to stand on.

Together with the completed-bridge card above, this pair documents the full arc of the crossing's construction, almost certainly during William Lynn's 1897 contract or the national-funds completion effort that followed — the primitive, vertiginous cableway method visible here standing in sharp contrast to the finished lattice-truss deck shown carrying a full mixed train in the companion plate.

PRG-003 February 1922

"The Railway to Costa Rica's Atlantic Coast Traverses Picturesque Country"

National Geographic Magazine, Vol. XLI, No. 2, p. 205

A full-page plate from Paul B. Popenoe's article "Costa Rica, Land of the Banana," published in The National Geographic Magazine, February 1922, page 205 — the same arched bridge seen completed in the Paynter Bros. postcard above, photographed from the gorge floor looking up at the span, with two men in the foreground pointing toward it for scale. The image is credited to the Central News Photo Service, and the caption beneath it reads in part that Costa Rica "now boasts 402 miles of railway, connecting its capital, San José, with both the Atlantic and Pacific ports."

Despite the caption's framing under a headline about the Atlantic-coast railway, Popenoe's article text contains no reference to this bridge at all — only a short general passage on the country's combined rail network. The picture and its caption are independent picture-desk copy, assembled separately from the article proper and illustrating the Pacific-line crossing under an Atlantic-themed headline, a mismatch traceable to the magazine's editorial process rather than to anything in Popenoe's reporting.

Puente Río Grande: The Pacific Line's Signature Crossing

Of all the engineering obstacles facing the Pacific line, none was more formidable than the descent from the Central Valley through Turrúcares and Cebadilla toward Atenas, where the route had to cross the gorge of the Río Grande. The solution, a 219.38-metre two-hinged deck truss arching roughly 102 to 105 metres above the river (sources differ slightly on the exact figure), was designed by the American civil engineer Theodore Cooper — twice awarded the Norman Medal by the American Society of Civil Engineers, and already known for railway bridges over the Allegheny, the Harlem, and the Merrimack rivers in the United States — and fabricated by the Milliken Bros. firm of New York. Work proceeded from both cliff faces simultaneously, the unsupported gap closed only at the end with center sections built last, using the kind of cantilevered erection arms and cableway-suspended work baskets visible in this spoke's construction-phase postcard. The bridge was completed in March 1902 at a reported cost of 131,981 Costa Rican gold pesos, under either William Lynn's original 1897 contract or the national-funds completion effort that followed his running out of financing in 1903.

The crossing's fame outlasted the line's working life by decades. Today it is formally listed on Costa Rica's Patrimonio Histórico-Arquitectónico register, under Executive Decree No. 30638-C of 2002, owned by the Instituto Costarricense de Ferrocarriles. Its visual drama — a slender steel arch suspended over a forested chasm — made it a natural subject for commercial photographers from the start: the Paynter Bros. studio in San José issued at least two postcards of the bridge, one documenting its construction and one its completed form carrying a full mixed train, and the crossing was picturesque enough to be recycled, two decades later, as wholly unrelated picture-desk illustration in a February 1922 National Geographic Magazine article about Costa Rica's banana economy — an article whose text, on inspection, says nothing whatsoever about the bridge itself, the photograph and its caption having been assembled independently of Paul Popenoe's reporting.

A second Pacific-line bridge in this catalogue, over the Río Virilla near Pavas (station No. 3), offers a useful documentary counterpart: a different river, a different publisher (Anto Lehmann rather than Paynter Bros.), and — uniquely among this collection's bridge cards — a personal inscription from an engineer named Guillermo, who wrote home to a sibling that he had personally directed the masonry work on that crossing. Together, the Río Grande and Virilla cards bracket the same essential story from opposite ends: one a commercially issued, anonymously photographed record of a famous public landmark, the other a private keepsake carrying a named individual's quiet claim to a smaller, less celebrated piece of the same railway's construction.
