Km 0.0 — station No. 1, the San José terminus of the Pacific line since 1910, rebuilt in 1941 to a design by José Francisco Salazar Quesada and declared a national historic-architectural landmark in 1997–98.

About this spoke: The San José terminus is the one station on this entire line that has been rebuilt at least once in a documented, dateable way — which makes it a natural place for this catalogue to show a station across two distinct buildings rather than one. The first plate shows the original terminus, an open-platform timber structure consistent with the line's early-1900s construction; a separate source places a Pacific station building at this site as of the 1920s. The second plate shows its 1941 replacement, designed by architect José Francisco Salazar Quesada in a rationalist-functionalist style, built in reinforced concrete at a cost of ¢484,346.83, and declared a national historic-architectural landmark in 1997–98. This catalogue has not confirmed whether the 1920s building shown here is itself the line's very first terminus structure from 1910 or an intermediate replacement — only that it predates 1941.

The Terminus, Two Buildings

SANJOSEP-001 Predates 1941

La Primera Estación

The original terminus, before the 1941 rebuild

A long, single-story timber building runs the length of the platform, its roof extending in a deep overhang supported on a colonnade of slender posts. At the left edge of the frame, a passenger coach stands under a separate train shed canopy, partly visible behind the platform building. Along the platform itself, a line of adults and children in early-twentieth-century dress walk toward the camera, with a low fence and a young tree enclosed in a protective frame in the foreground. The image is captioned simply "primera estación" — the first station.

No precise construction date for this specific building survives among the sources consulted for this catalogue, though a separate archival caption places a Pacific station building at this same site as of the 1920s, and the line itself had reached full completion to Puntarenas in 1910. Whether this structure was the very first terminus building erected at the time of that 1910 completion, or an intermediate replacement built sometime in the following decade, is not something this catalogue can resolve with the evidence at hand.

SANJOSEP-002 Built 1941

The 1941 Terminus

Salazar Quesada's rationalist-functionalist replacement, still standing today

A clean-lined, reinforced concrete building presents its façade to a row of period automobiles parked in close formation along the front. The structure's rounded corners, stepped massing, and a low tower element at the center read clearly as Art Decó and rationalist-functionalist design, a sharp departure from the wooden platform shed of the earlier terminus. The cars parked in front, by their styling, place this photograph in the years following the building's construction, most plausibly the 1940s.

This building replaced the earlier wooden terminus entirely, constructed during the administration of Rafael Ángel Calderón Guardia (1940–1944) to a design by architect José Francisco Salazar Quesada. It remains standing today, still in active use, and was declared a national Patrimonio Histórico-Arquitectónico landmark in the late 1990s.

One Terminus, Two Buildings

The Pacific line's San José terminus has stood at the same site in the city's Hospital district, at the corner now formally named Avenida Panamá and Calle 2, since the line's full completion to Puntarenas on 23 July 1910. But the building that has occupied that site has not stayed the same. The first plate in this spoke shows an open-platform timber structure, simply captioned "primera estación" — and while no source consulted here confirms an exact construction date for it, an archival caption elsewhere places a Pacific station building at this address as of the 1920s, well before the replacement that most visitors to San José would recognize today.

That replacement came in 1941, during the presidency of Rafael Ángel Calderón Guardia, to a design by the architect José Francisco Salazar Quesada (1892–1968). Built entirely in reinforced concrete at a reported cost of ¢484,346.83, the new station ran 731.5 square metres across two floors — administrative offices, a waiting room, and the Jefatura de Transportes upstairs; a vestibule, ticket sales, baggage, telegraph, and the guards' headquarters below. Architecturally, it belongs squarely to the rationalist-functionalist movement: pure volumes, an absence of applied ornament, broad glazed façades using glass block as a then-cutting-edge material, organized around a central two-story vestibule with curved staircases rising to the upper floor. Along with the railway's old workshop buildings nearby, it stands today as one of the clearest examples of industrial modern architecture in Costa Rica, formally recognized as a national landmark of historic and architectural interest on 26 November 1997, with the broader railway complex declared Patrimonio Histórico-Arquitectónico under Decree 26552-C on 9 January 1998.

Today the building still serves its original purpose in part — it houses INCOFER's main offices and remains an active stop on the Tren Interurbano, the modern urban passenger service that now runs over much of the old Pacific line's right-of-way. Alongside the building itself, the surrounding railway complex preserves the passenger platform, the old Talleres (workshop) building, a fountain, a bust of former president Rafael Iglesias Castro, and the two preserved steam locomotives "María Cecilia" and "Gandoca" — together forming, in the words of one source consulted for this catalogue, a single urban landmark of historic and architectural value for the city of San José.
