Km 91.5 — station No. 43 on the Pacific line, fixed to the fishing settlement and mangrove estuary that today gives the place its name, two stops short of the line's terminus on the coast at Puntarenas via Caldera.

About this spoke: Mata de Limón carries no formal Patrimonio designation and no heritage adaptive-reuse story — its significance is geographic rather than architectural. Station No. 43 sat at the point where the line, and later the coastal road, had to thread a living mangrove estuary fed by the Cambalache stream (itself the namesake of station No. 40, just up the line) on its final approach to the port works at Caldera. This spoke gathers a single dated plate of the steel truss bridge that carries the crossing today, set against the estuary's mangrove fringe and the Caldera port works visible on the horizon.

The Estero Bridge

Steel truss crossing, mangrove estuary, and the Caldera waterfront

A modern steel truss bridge, carried on two tall lattice towers, spans the Mata de Limón estuary at low tide, with mudflats and braided tidal channels exposed on both sides of the crossing. A dense mangrove fringe runs along the far bank, separating the estuary from the open water of the Gulf of Nicoya beyond; the silhouettes of the Caldera port's cranes, breakwater, and moored cargo ships are visible on the horizon at upper left, with a freighter and several smaller vessels riding at anchor offshore. In the near foreground, a two-story house with a small pool sits among palms on the estuary's near bank, and the cleared sandy approach to a footpath is visible at lower right, giving a sense of the settlement's intimate scale against the working port beyond it.

The bridge pictured here is a modern road and pedestrian crossing rather than a railway structure; no original Ferrocarril al Pacífico station-house photograph for Mata de Limón has yet entered this archive. What the plate documents instead is the geography the line's builders had to contend with at km 91.5 — a living, tidally flushed mangrove estuary, fed by the Cambalache stream named for station No. 40 a few kilometers up the line, sitting directly across the route's final approach to Caldera and the Pacific coast.

Mata de Limón: Where the Line Met the Estuary

Mata de Limón does not appear in any list of the Pacific line's heritage-protected stations, and no adaptive-reuse story attaches to it the way one does at Orotina or Atenas. Its interest for this catalogue is geographic rather than architectural: at km 91.5, station No. 43 sat at the point where William Lynn's original 1897 construction contract, and the national-funds effort that completed the line after 1903, had to carry the railbed across a living tidal mangrove system rather than simply through open countryside. The estuary takes its principal freshwater supply from the Cambalache stream — the same watercourse that gives station No. 40, a few kilometers back up the line toward San José, its name — supplemented by smaller intermittent streams during the rainy season that runs roughly from May through November along this stretch of Pacific coast.

The settlement that grew up around the crossing eventually outgrew its identity as a mere station stop: in 2014 the Costa Rican Legislative Assembly created Mata de Limón as a district in its own right, the head of the Caldera district within the canton of Esparza, Puntarenas province. Today it is described as a small fishing village built around the estuary, known for its mangrove wildlife — including the fiddler crab, whose asymmetrical claws are a frequently noted feature of the estero's tidal flats — and for proximity to the modern container port and cruise terminal complex at Caldera, whose cranes and breakwater are visible across the water in this plate.

The crossing has not been a passive backdrop to that development. Environmental surveys of the Mata de Limón mangrove forest identify the original construction of the railway line at the end of the nineteenth century, together with the later coastal highway and the Caldera port complex, as the principal disturbances that have fragmented the wetland's connectivity to the dry tropical forest behind it and altered its natural sediment and tidal-flushing patterns. The steel bridge pictured here — a road and pedestrian crossing rather than a railway structure — is itself part of that layered infrastructure history, sitting on or near the corridor the original line once threaded through the mangroves on its final approach to Caldera before the Pacific line's 1995 closure. A separate suspension footbridge project across the estero, advanced by Costa Rica's Pacific port authority (INCOP) in recent years, points to the crossing's continuing importance to the divided community on either bank.
