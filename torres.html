Identification confirmed: The AtoM archival description for this plate reads “Fotografía de la línea férrea en un lugar cerca del Río Torres. A los lados se observan casas. San José” — a photograph of the railway line near the Río Torres, houses visible on both sides, San José. This corrects an earlier reading of the inscribed caption, which named only “Torres” and was provisionally treated as an unidentified location. The Río Torres is the river that gives its name to Puente Río Torres, the crossing noted in the hub table just past Pavas (station No. 3, km 6.3) on the earliest stretch of the Pacific line leaving San José. This places the photograph at the opposite end of the route from where it was first catalogued — not in the Puntarenas lowlands, but within the city of San José itself, in a residential district along the river.

TOR-01 26 January 1923

Línea Férrea cerca del Río Torres

San José · near station No. 3 (Pavas), km 6.3 · 26 January 1923

Caption inscribed on the print itself reads simply Torres 1/26/23; the fuller AtoM archival description identifies the subject precisely: “Fotografía de la línea férrea en un lugar cerca del Río Torres. A los lados se observan casas. San José.” The Río Torres flows through the northern districts of San José and gives its name to the bridge crossing noted in this catalogue’s hub table immediately after Pavas (station No. 3, km 6.3) — among the earliest stretches of the Pacific line leaving the capital, well before the Puntarenas-lowland sections documented in most of the rest of this survey.

The photograph shows a man in a broad-brimmed hat walking along the track, facing the camera mid-stride. What had initially read as rustic worksite structures — a thatched-roof building at left and a small gabled wooden building at right — are, in this corrected context, ordinary urban-edge dwellings (casas) of the kind that lined the railway right-of-way through San José’s outer neighbourhoods in 1923. Loose construction or maintenance materials lie beside the track in the middle distance. The setting is consistent with a residential district at the city’s edge, where modest houses backed directly onto the line as it made its way out toward Sabana and Pavas.

Dated 26 January 1923, this plate falls within the same week as the Escobal (24 Jan.) and Esparta (27 Jan.) plates documented elsewhere in this catalogue — but rather than placing the survey team in the Puntarenas lowlands that week, it indicates the January 1923 programme also covered the line’s San José end during the same general period. The survey’s geographic logic across January 1923 therefore appears to have ranged across multiple sections of the line rather than proceeding in a single continuous sweep from one end to the other.

The source print is of noticeably lower resolution than most other plates in this collection, suggesting either a different negative generation, a smaller original format, or a later, lower-quality reproduction.
